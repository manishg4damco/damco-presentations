Diagnosis First

Speaker Manish Gupta Date May 1, 2026 Duration 7–8 minutes Position Second speaker, following Mohit
What This Talk Covers and Why

Damco used to walk into client conversations leading with what we can build. We shifted to leading with understanding the real problem first. That one change, diagnosis before proposal, is how trust is built from day zero, how budget stops being an obstacle, and how Damco is maturing in how it engages the market.

The 5 Changes, Manish's Framework

These are the specific changes Manish introduced to the Damco pre-sales approach. Named explicitly in Beat 4.

Change 1
Diagnosis First
Every conversation starts with the real problem, before capability, solution, or price.
Change 2
Trust from Day Zero
Trust is built in the first conversation, not after the contract. The client should feel understood before they feel sold to.
Change 3
Budget as Design Constraint
If the pain is real, a tailored solution always exists. Budget tells us how to sequence, not whether to engage.
Change 4
Clarity of Fit
The client should leave every conversation knowing why Damco is the right partner, because the conversation showed it.
Change 5
Business Value Orientation
Every solution is framed in terms of what changes for the business. The output is a capability. The outcome is the point.

The Complete Script
Beat 1 Name the Old Way 50 sec

"I want to start by being honest about something."

"For most of our history, and this is true of most IT services firms, the first thing we did when a client came to us was tell them what we could build. We led with capability. We showed the portfolio. We put together a proposal. Sometimes we won the work. Sometimes we didn't. And when we didn't, we usually told ourselves it was about price."

"It wasn't always about price."

Note: I will open with "I want to start by being honest." The final line, "It wasn't always about price," is a full stop. I will pause and let it land.
Beat 2 RJ-Legal: Making the Solution Real 90 sec

"About two months ago I was on a call with a lawyer in Australia. He had a product idea, his exact words: 'It's just in my head.' He described his workflow, his clients' pain, his vision for a SaaS platform. No written brief, no formal budget, no other vendors in the conversation. And the old instinct would have been: take notes, come back with a proposal, show him what we can do."

"I did something different. Before the second call, I built a prototype. Not a polished product, a working visual of what he had described. His portal. The compliance workflow. The way lawyers and clients would move through the system. His business model on a screen."

"When he saw it on the second call, something changed. He stopped explaining his idea. He started validating it. He added requirements we hadn't discussed. By the end of the call, he said: 'Let's start. Send me what I need to sign.'"

"He didn't say yes because our price was right. He said yes because his vision existed outside his head for the first time. Trust was built in two conversations, before a single line of contract. That is diagnosis first."

Note: I will pause on "That is diagnosis first." This is the pivot of the talk.
Beat 3 Hoop Messenger + ABNAH: Two More Expressions 115 sec

"That approach doesn't always mean building a prototype. Sometimes it means something different, a diagnosis so clear that the client finally understands their own situation, and a commitment so complete that they stop wondering if it can be done."

"Sahand Adilipour had a secure messaging application, ten years old, completely shut down. No users. No one maintaining it. Other developers had looked at it. Nobody had committed to fixing it. When Damco came back to him, we came with a full assessment. Every layer of that codebase. Every reason it was broken. Exactly what it would take to bring it back to life, in what sequence, at what cost."

"And then we did something the others hadn't. We took complete ownership. Not 'here is what we found.' We will see this through."

"Sahand had not seen that combination before. The depth of the diagnosis. The confidence behind the commitment. The application went live."

"The same pattern shows up at enterprise scale too. Aditya Birla New Age Hospitality, ABNAH, needed a digital platform for their café business across India. Before any contract, before a formal brief, Damco built out the complete picture. The user experience. The architecture. Every integration, laid out in full. Not a proposal, a vision of exactly what their platform could look like. They had not seen another vendor go that deep before the conversation was even formal. Damco became their sole technology partner."

"Three different clients. Three different situations. The same question at the centre of each one: do we understand this problem deeply enough to make it real for the client before we ask them to sign anything?"

"And these are not isolated stories, Tamarind, Visaka, the same pattern, different industries, the same outcome: clients who trusted us with the work before we had finished explaining the price."

Note: I will keep the ABNAH story short: three sentences of setup, one line of outcome. I will not expand it in the room. The Tamarind/Visaka line I will deliver in one breath and move on.
Beat 4 The 5 Changes 110 sec

"So what actually changed? Let me be specific, because this is not a mindset shift that is difficult to describe. These are five concrete things."

Change 1: Diagnosis First

"First: diagnosis before proposal. The first conversation is not a pitch. It is a diagnostic. Before we talk about what we can build, we need to understand what the client is actually trying to solve. Not what they've described, what they are trying to solve. Those are often different things."

Change 2: Trust from Day Zero

"Second: trust from day zero. Trust is not earned after the contract is signed. It is built, or lost, in the first conversation. When a client feels genuinely understood, when they sense that we are thinking about their problem and not our pipeline, that is when trust starts. The first call is already delivery."

Change 3: Budget as Design Constraint

"Third: budget is a design constraint, not a barrier. If the pain is real, genuinely real, there is always a tailored solution. Budget tells us what to build first and how to sequence it. It does not tell us whether to engage. Every client, wherever they sit, deserves a solution shaped around where they are."

Change 4: Clarity of Fit

"Fourth: clarity of fit. At the end of every conversation, the client should feel that Damco is the right partner. Not because we said so, because the quality of the conversation showed it. When you go deep on someone's problem before they have asked you to, that demonstrates capability more than any portfolio ever will."

Change 5: Business Value, Not Technical Output

"And fifth: business value, not technical output. We are not selling code. We are selling what changes for the business when the right solution is in place. Every proposal, every scoping conversation, framed in terms of outcomes. What does this unlock? What does this stop hurting? That is the conversation clients remember."

Note: I will not say "change number one, change number two." The rhythm carries the sequence. I will deliver each change as a personal observation, not a list item.
Beat 5 Microsoft Clarity: Listening After the Meeting 40 sec

"And this does not stop when the call ends. After we share a proposal or a deck with a client, we can see, through behavioral analytics, how they moved through it. Which sections they spent time on. What they came back to on the second read. What they skipped in three seconds."

"We use Microsoft Clarity for this. It tells us what matters to the client even when they don't say it. So the next conversation starts exactly where their attention is, not where we assumed it would be."

Beat 6 What It Unlocks: The Maturity Story 75 sec

"A firm that leads with capability is telling clients: trust us because of what we can do. A firm that leads with diagnosis is telling clients: trust us because of how well we understand you. That is a different kind of firm. That is what Dimple is pointing at when she says this is not just a sales change."

For Clients

"For clients, they get a partner who understood their problem before they signed anything. Not a vendor who bid on the version of the problem they described."

For Damco

"For Damco, we win work that fits. We scope it cleanly. We build trust before the contract is signed, and that trust holds through delivery because what was promised was grounded in reality from the start."

For You

"For the people in this room, the skill being built here is not a sales skill. It is a professional skill. The ability to sit across from someone, understand their real problem, and make it tangible, before you propose anything, is what separates good practitioners from exceptional ones. It is learnable. It is what we are building."

"The best work we have won at Damco was won before we wrote a single line of proposal."
